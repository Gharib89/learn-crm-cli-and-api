Milestone 12 · Capstone · Lesson 03

Wire the
relationships

One write turns two standalone tables into a parent and a child: a 1:N from Account to Deal Review, with the lookup that points each review back at its account. Then prove it by creating a review that's actually attached.

live on agent-cloud  ·  writes your turn
Why this matters: The brief says reviews "hang off the Account." That's a relationship — and it's why this step had to wait: a 1:N command needs both tables to already exist. Get the direction right (Account is the one, Deal Review is the many) and the lookup lands on the right table; get it backwards and you'd be putting accounts under reviews.

A relationship in Dataverse is a column. Creating a 1:N from Account to Deal Review adds a lookup column to Deal Review — so "the relationship" and "the lookup" are the same write, not two.

01 The runbook

Three steps: create the 1:N (which creates the lookup), verify it's on the child, then create a real Deal Review bound to an account to prove the wiring carries data.

GOAL Account 1:N Deal Review, with a working itworx_accountid lookup.

target: agent-cloud · both tables must already exist (L02) · into DealDesk

  1. 1. Create the 1:N relationship. --referenced-entity is the one side (account); --referencing-entity is the many side (deal review) that receives the lookup.

    step 1 · POST /RelationshipDefinitionscrm metadata create-one-to-many \
  --schema-name itworx_account_dealreview \
  --referenced-entity account --referencing-entity itworx_dealreview \
  --lookup-schema itworx_accountid --lookup-display "Account" \
  --solution DealDesk --publish

    ✓ verify: crm metadata relationships itworx_dealreview lists itworx_account_dealreview as a many-to-one on the child.

  2. 2. Confirm the lookup column. The relationship created a lookup attribute on Deal Review — it should be in the entity's attribute list.

    step 2 · read-backcrm metadata entity itworx_dealreview # find itworx_accountid (Lookup)

    ✓ verify: itworx_accountid appears with type Lookup, targeting account.

  3. 3. Create a Deal Review bound to an account. The @odata.bind on the lookup's navigation property is how you attach a child to its parent at create time (M05 L04). Use any account GUID from your org.

    step 3 · POST /itworx_dealreviewscrm entity create itworx_dealreviews --data '{
  "itworx_name": "Acme — Q3 deal",
  "itworx_status": 1,
  "itworx_AccountId@odata.bind": "/accounts(<account-guid>)"
}'

    ✓ verify: crm entity get itworx_dealreviews <new-guid> --expand itworx_AccountId returns the linked account — the wiring carries data.

02 Direction is the whole game

A 1:N is asymmetric. The dry-run body names the two roles explicitly — read them before committing:

live · crm --dry-run metadata create-one-to-many …"@odata.type": "…OneToManyRelationshipMetadata",
"ReferencedEntity": "account",      # the ONE (parent)
"ReferencingEntity": "itworx_dealreview",  # the MANY (child, gets the lookup)
"Lookup": { "SchemaName": "itworx_accountid" }
REFERENCED = ONE
Mnemonic: the referenced entity is the one being pointed at — the parent, the "one". The referencing entity does the pointing — the child, the "many", and it's where the lookup column is born. Swap them and you've modeled "an account belongs to a review", which is nonsense for this brief.

03 The cascade default you inherited

You didn't pass any --cascade-* flags, so the relationship took referential defaults. The one worth knowing for a Deal Desk:

BehaviorDefaultMeans
cascade-deleteRemoveLinkDelete an account → its Deal Reviews survive, just unlinked. (Not Cascade, which would delete the children too.)
cascade-assign / shareNoCascadeReassigning or sharing the account does not ripple to its reviews.
DELIBERATE
For a review/approval record, RemoveLink is usually right — you rarely want deleting a customer to silently destroy its audit trail of reviews. If the brief wanted parental cascade, you'd pass --cascade-delete Cascade. Either way, choose it; don't discover it. (Full cascade matrix: M05 L03.)

Lesson check

4 questions · click to reveal

1. In a 1:N Account → Deal Review, which table gets the lookup column?

Correct. The referencing (many) entity receives the lookup — Deal Review points back at its account. A join/intersect table is the N:N story, not 1:N.
The referencing (many) side — Deal Review — gets the lookup. Account is referenced (the one); only the child carries the lookup, and 1:N has no join table.

2. Why couldn't this relationship be created back in L02?

Correct. create-one-to-many references both account and itworx_dealreview — the child table had to exist first. That dependency is why relationships sit after schema in the chain.
It's the dependency: both referenced and referencing tables must exist before the 1:N can bind them. It's not about solutions, the option set, or any CLI limit.

3. How do you attach a new Deal Review to an account at create time?

Correct. The @odata.bind on the lookup's navigation property links child to parent in the same POST — no second call needed. That's the M05 L04 child-via-lookup pattern.
Bind the lookup navigation property with @odata.bind in the create body. A plain name string won't link it, and you don't need a second update or associate call for a 1:N at create time.

4. You ran no --cascade-* flags. Delete an account — what happens to its reviews?

Correct. The referential default for cascade-delete is RemoveLink — children survive, just unlinked. To delete them with the parent you'd opt into --cascade-delete Cascade explicitly.
The default is RemoveLink: reviews survive, unlinked. Cascade (delete children) and Restrict (block) are opt-in, not the default.
